Welcome!

Are you looking to learn how to use Krita to paint digitally? If so, you've come to the right place! This tutorial will teach you the basics of Krita by guiding you through a painting demo.

Scroll to begin!

If you prefer to learn Krita's tools by reading about them, click on any of the links to the right to learn the basics about a specific tool.

Note that this website is not intended to teach you how to paint well. Rather, this website is just a tool for you to learn the basic tools necessary to start painting.

Painting Demo

This is a step-by-step guide on how to use a few of Krita's tools to create a digital painting. I will teach you the basics starting from creating a document. For this demo, I will be painting a picture of a leopard. I encourage you to follow along or make your own painting!

Creating a new document

We start by creating a new document and specifying how big we want out painting to be. For this painting, I will use a 3000px by 2000px document with a 300ppi.

Note that while larger documents will produce clearer paintings due to more pixels, setting your document too large may create lag when painting if you're working on an older machine. It's easier to scale down a painting, but making a painting larger will almost always make it more blurry. I suggest starting at 3000px by 2000px and going from there.

After that, simply click "Create" to create your document.

Selecting a brush

You should now be presented by a white canvas. To create our rough sketch, select the brush tool in the toolbar on the left side of the screen, which looks like a paintbrush.

Now we will choose what our brush's mark looks like. Click on the icon on the top of the screen that has four small squares in it. This is where we can choose a brush preset. You can experiment to see what brush you like, but I usually just use the basic opacity brush.

A couple more things! We need to choose what size we want our brush to be along with the color. You can use the slider at the top of the screen labeled,"Size," to choose a brush size that you like. I will be using a size of 15px along with the color black that I selected using the color wheel at the top right.

Rough sketch

Now we are ready to sketch! I will first create a rough sketch of my leopard. While painting, you can use the + and - keys on your keyboard to zoom in and out. You can also hold down the spacebar and drag with your mouse to pan across the canvas.

After creating my rough sketch, I realized the leopard's head is too small. To make it bigger, we can select that "Freehand Selection Tool" in the toolbar and draw a selection around the area of the leopard we want to resize. Then, we can select the "Transform a layer or selection" tool and resize the selection and move it around to our liking. Hold Shift if you want to maintain the same proportions when resizing. When you're finished, press Enter. Then deselect everything by going to the "Select" dropdown at the top left of the screen and click "Deselect."

One trick I like to use when sketching and painting is to periodically flip the canvas horizontally, which makes it easier to spot mistakes. To do this, click the Image dropdown at the top left of the screen and click "Mirror Image Horizontally."

Refined sketch

For the refined sketch, I like to turn down the opacity, or "see-throughness" of the rough sketch layer and the size of my brush. Make sure to create a new layer so that if you want to erase or change something, you can do so without affecting the rough sketch layer. You can also rename your layers by double clicking the name, which will help you later on if you end up with many layers.

Base color

Now we are ready to add our inital layer of color. Create a new layer and move it below your two sketch layers so that your sketches show up above the colors. You can do this by simply dragging the layer to where you want it to be. I usually like to paint my initial layer as if there is no light or shadow on my subject.

Feel free to experiment with different brush presents and sizes.

For the leopard's spots, I want to be able to only draw on top of the base color so I don't color outside the lines. To do this, I will group the spots and base color layer by holding Shift and selecting them and pressing Ctrl + G to group them together. This doesn't do much by itself, but now I will enable the "Inherit Alpha" icon that looks like cursive "a" icon to the right of the spots layer. Now, whatever I color on the spots layer will be limited to the areas I already colored on the base color layer.

Adding shadows and light

After painting the base color, I will start adding shadows and highlights by changing the layers' blend mode. Blend modes are a little complicated, but they basically change how colors on a layer look based on the colors on the layers underneath it.

First, I will create a layer named "shadow" and another layer named "light." Again, will enable the "Inherit Alpha" icon on both layers because I don't paint outside the base color. Make sure these layers are in the layer group we made earlier.

For the shadow layer, I will change the blend mode of the layer to "Multiply" from the dropdown at the top of the layers panel. Now you can paint in where I want the shadows to be.

For the light layer, the blend mode will be "Overlay," and I will use a light yellowish-orange color to paint in where I want the light to be. After painting in the light, I realize that I want the light to be even stronger and brighter, so I will just duplicate the light layer by selecting it and clicking the second icon at the bottom of the layers panel.

At this point, you can make your rough sketch layer invisible by clicking the eye icon to the left of the layer if it's not helping you much. I will turn it off but keep the refined sketch layer on until I paint in the details.

Painting details

Our leopard looks pretty good! Now I want to add details such as painting in fur textures in certain areas and adding more details to the eyes and nose.

Create a layer with the normal blend mode. Now, simply paint where you want more details!

Adding a background

We are pretty much finished. However, I want to create a blurry background. To do this, I will first add a new layer and place it underneath everything. After loosely painting a background, I will go up to "Filter" dropdown in the top menu, hover over "Blur," and click "Gaussian Blur."

Make sure that "Preview" is checked so that you can see the effect of the blur while you move the slider. When your horizontal and vertical radius is at a place you like, click "OK."

If you are unsure whether or not you will want to keep the blur, select "Create Filter Mask" instead of "OK," which creates the blur on a new layer that you can delete later on if you want.
